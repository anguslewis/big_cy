# Strand 2 — Models of the International Financial System with Demand for Safe Assets / Convenience Yields

**Annotated bibliography.** Survey of how open-economy models that turn on safe-asset / convenience demand are *built* and how they *relate*. For each entry we record:

- **(a) Demand for safe asset:** EXOGENOUS (utility/preference shock, bonds-in-utility, reduced-form clientele/noise demand) vs. ENDOGENOUS (collateral, intermediary constraints, search, settlement liquidity).
- **(b) Economy:** closed vs. open; number of countries; periods/horizon.
- **(c) Source of convenience:** what gives the dollar / safe asset its special value.
- **(d) Implications:** portfolios / NFA composition / risk premia / exchange rates / carry.
- **(e) Relative vs. absolute convenience:** does the wedge measure a *gap* between near-substitutes (relative) or an *absolute* level on safe assets?
- **Proximity to big_cy (1–5):** 5 = directly competes; 1 = background/foundational.

Proximity is calibrated to big_cy's thesis: that the *absolute* magnitude of the convenience yield on safe assets is large (DiTella-style 2–4%) and can microfound home-currency bias and resolve the US-carry/risk-tolerance tension in a Kekre-Lenel-type model.

## CATEGORY A — Directly related: structural open-economy models with convenience/safety demand on dollar assets

### Kekre & Lenel (2024), "The Flight to Safety and International Risk Sharing," *AER* 114(6):1650–1691.
The central structural template for big_cy. A two-country (Home=US / Foreign=RoW) New Keynesian business-cycle model with sticky nominal wages and capital, augmented with two ingredients: (i) a **time-varying nonpecuniary "convenience" value on safe dollar bonds** entered bonds-in-utility (BIU) à la Sidrauski / Krishnamurthy-Vissing-Jorgensen, driven by exogenous **"safety shocks"**; and (ii) **cross-country heterogeneity in risk-bearing capacity** (Home is more risk-tolerant, via Epstein-Zin risk aversion). A flight to safety (positive safety shock) appreciates the dollar and lowers global output; dollar bonds become an endogenous hedge earning a *negative* risk premium, and the US optimally holds a levered long-capital / short-dollar portfolio (the "world insurer"). Calibrated to the Du-Im-Schreger Treasury basis (safety shock volatility), the global equity premium, and the equity-sensitivity of US NFA. Safety shocks explain >25% of US output volatility; heterogeneity in risk-bearing capacity accounts for essentially all of the positive average return on the US external position. Resolves Maggiori's "reserve-currency paradox" (dollar appreciates in bad times *with* consumption home bias) because the safety shock is a demand shock for a *type of asset within* the dollar, not a currency risk-premium shock. Closing section: dollar swap lines act like a negative safety shock (supply of safe dollar liquidity), stimulating globally but revaluing wealth toward the US.
- (a) **EXOGENOUS** — bonds-in-utility, time-varying safety shock (latent demand). Supply also matters (swap lines).
- (b) Open, 2-country, infinite horizon, business-cycle frequency.
- (c) Nonpecuniary value of safe *dollar* bonds (Treasuries/money-like); foreigners and US both value them but demand is calibrated to dollar-bond spreads.
- (d) US runs levered long-capital/short-dollar portfolio; dollar = negative-beta hedge; safety shocks drive global comovement of output, NFA, excess bond returns; positive average return on US NFA from risk-tolerance gap.
- (e) **Relative** as implemented: the convenience yield is disciplined by the Treasury *basis* (a near-substitute spread), so the calibrated wedge is the relative one — this is exactly the lever big_cy proposes to crank toward the *absolute* DiTella level.
- **Proximity: 5.**

### Jiang, Krishnamurthy & Lustig (2024), "Dollar Safety and the Global Financial Cycle," *ReStud* 91(5):2878–2915 (NBER WP 27682, 2020; first version 2021).
A model of the **global financial cycle as a dollar cycle**, built on one key asymmetry: **foreign investors are willing to pay a convenience yield to hold safe dollar bonds**, with a convenience-demand function *decreasing in the quantity of dollar bonds outstanding*. Couples this exchange-rate block with a production/financing side: firms worldwide face a standard credit constraint (debt capacity in future revenue) and tilt liabilities toward dollars to capture the convenience yield. Generates six facts: dollar funding advantage (Fact 1), dollar debt dominance (Fact 2), flight-to-dollar safety (Fact 3), the US-centric global financial cycle (Fact 4), US exorbitant privilege as a levered carry position (Fact 5), and the dollar as a global risk factor (Fact 6). US monetary tightening reduces the *supply* of dollar bonds (credit channel), raising the convenience yield and appreciating the dollar beyond the standard UIP channel — the novel transmission mechanism. The convenience-yield (seigniorage) channel, not insurance/risk-bearing, is the engine; explicitly argues this resolves the reserve-currency paradox without needing US risk tolerance.
- (a) **EXOGENOUS** dollar convenience demand (decreasing in dollar-bond quantity); **ENDOGENOUS** supply via firm financing under collateral constraints.
- (b) Open, US/RoW, infinite horizon.
- (c) Foreign investors' nonpecuniary value for safe *dollar* claims; quantity-dependent so scarcity raises it.
- (d) Dollar appreciates when convenience demand rises; US issues cheap dollar safe debt (privilege); GFC = dollar cycle driven by US monetary policy via dollar-bond supply.
- (e) **Relative** (Treasury/LIBOR basis is the measured wedge) but the framing — a quantity-dependent demand curve for *the* safe dollar asset — is conceptually closest to an absolute notion; the seigniorage size depends on the level.
- **Proximity: 5.** Closest competitor/cousin to big_cy's mechanism (convenience drives privilege without risk-tolerance).

### Jiang, Krishnamurthy & Lustig (2021), "Foreign Safe Asset Demand and the Dollar Exchange Rate," *JF* 76(3):1049–1089 (NBER WP 24439, 2018).
The empirical/theoretical foundation for the JKL program. Develops a **convenience-yield theory of the exchange rate**: the dollar's FX valuation equals the expected present value of future interest-rate differentials *minus* future currency risk premia *minus* the convenience yield foreigners derive from US safe assets. Derives the dollar level as a function of current and future foreign convenience yields, measured by the **Treasury basis** (yield gap between US Treasuries and currency-hedged foreign government bonds). Estimates foreigners earn ~2% extra convenience yield on Treasuries, ~90% of it attributable to the *dollar denomination* rather than Treasury-specific safety/liquidity. A 1-SD rise in convenience yield widens the basis ~20bp and appreciates the dollar ~3% over two quarters, reversing over 2–3 years. Convenience accounts for 16–28% of quarterly dollar variation; helps resolve the exchange-rate disconnect.
- (a) **EXOGENOUS** (reduced-form convenience demand for dollar safe assets, measured via the basis).
- (b) Open; pricing-kernel / no-arbitrage exchange-rate framework (not a full GE).
- (c) Foreign safety/liquidity demand for dollar-denominated safe claims; 90% is the *dollar* per se.
- (d) Dollar appreciates with convenience; helps explain disconnect, UIP violations; lends support to "special US" theories.
- (e) **Relative** — the convenience yield is literally the Treasury basis (Treasury vs. hedged-foreign-govt), a near-substitute gap. big_cy's point of departure.
- **Proximity: 5.**

### Maggiori (2017), "Financial Intermediation, International Risk Sharing, and Reserve Currencies," *AER* 107(10):3038–3071.
The reserve-currency-paradox paper. A two-country model where countries differ in **financial development**: the key country's (US) intermediaries can raise funding even when poorly capitalized, so they take more leveraged risk; RoW intermediaries accumulate precautionary safe long positions. In good times the US consumes more and runs a trade deficit financed by financial income for bearing risk; in crises it transfers wealth to RoW (insurance pays out), and its currency appreciates as a safety hedge — making the dollar the reserve currency. The **"reserve-currency paradox"**: with consumption home bias, an insurance/risk-bearing story predicts the reserve currency should *depreciate* in crises (to run the trade surplus that pays out the insurance), counter to data. Identifies financial net worth of intermediaries as the crucial state variable.
- (a) **ENDOGENOUS** — convenience/safety arises from intermediary financial-constraint asymmetry, not utility. (No BIU.)
- (b) Open, 2-country, infinite horizon; intermediary-based.
- (c) Dollar's safety from the US's superior financial intermediation depth (risk-bearing), not a utility convenience.
- (d) US = levered risky-asset holder / safe-debt issuer; transfers wealth in crises; reserve currency appreciates in crises.
- (e) **Absolute-ish on risk-bearing, but no convenience wedge per se** — the "safety premium" is a risk price, not a near-substitute basis. The paradox it raises is exactly what KL2024 / JKL2024 / big_cy aim to resolve.
- **Proximity: 4.**

### Devereux, Engel & Wu (2023), "Collateral Advantage: Exchange Rates, Capital Flows and Global Cycles," NBER WP 31164.
A two-country NK model in which **US government debt has an advantage as superior collateral on bank balance sheets**. In global stress, the convenience (collateral) value of US bonds rises, the dollar appreciates *despite* US absorption falling relative to RoW — addressing the reserve-currency paradox via a collateral channel. Links exchange rates, capital flows, and the global financial cycle to the collateral premium on US debt.
- (a) **ENDOGENOUS** — convenience arises from collateral usefulness in bank balance sheets (constraint-based), with a time-varying collateral premium.
- (b) Open, 2-country, NK.
- (c) Collateral/safety value of US government debt for banks.
- (d) Dollar appreciates in downturns; convenience yield on US bonds rises; resolves reserve-currency paradox.
- (e) **Absolute-leaning** — a collateral premium on the safe asset level, though disciplined against spreads.
- **Proximity: 4.**

### Bianchi, Bigio & Engel (2023), "Scrambling for Dollars: International Liquidity, Banks and Exchange Rates," NBER WP 29457 (CEPR DP 16712).
Exchange-rate theory from **banks' precautionary demand for dollar liquidity**. Interbank settlement frictions mean unpredictable flows can leave banks "scrambling for dollars," generating an **endogenous convenience yield on the dollar**. When dollar funding risk rises, banks demand dollar assets and the dollar appreciates. Evidence linking G10 exchange rates to dollar-liquidity quantities.
- (a) **ENDOGENOUS** — convenience emerges from settlement/liquidity frictions and precautionary reserve demand.
- (b) Open, banking-centered.
- (c) Dollar's role as interbank settlement / reserve liquidity asset.
- (d) Dollar appreciates with funding risk; convenience yield is state-dependent; ties FX to liquidity quantities.
- (e) **Absolute** — an endogenous level of dollar convenience, not a basis gap.
- **Proximity: 4.**

## CATEGORY B — Same family (intermediary / financier portfolio-balance), convenience implicit

### Gabaix & Maggiori (2015), "International Liquidity and Exchange Rate Dynamics," *QJE* 130(3):1369–1420.
The canonical **portfolio-balance / financier** model of FX determination under imperfect financial markets. Global financiers with **limited risk-bearing capacity** (Γ) intermediate currency imbalances; their balance-sheet constraint (proportional to a credit-diversion friction) gives a finite, downward-sloping demand for currency risk. Gross flows move the exchange rate because financiers must be compensated to absorb them: a demand shift appreciates the currency today, expected to depreciate. Nests UIP (Γ=0) and financial autarky (Γ→∞); generates UIP deviations, exchange-rate disconnect, and (with α>0) reconciles CIP holding while UIP fails. Flexible: nests nontradables, sticky prices, production, FX intervention. The Maggiori (2022) handbook chapter builds its unified map around this.
- (a) **EXOGENOUS** currency demand shocks / noise demand; no convenience utility. Constraint is reduced-form.
- (b) Open, 2-country, 2-period (extensible).
- (c) No explicit dollar convenience; segmentation + risk-bearing limits generate currency risk premia.
- (d) Gross flows → exchange rates; UIP violations; carry tied to financier risk-bearing; disconnect.
- (e) **Relative** (currency risk premium between two currencies); convenience absent.
- **Proximity: 3.** Methodological backbone; big_cy's KL template descends from this lineage but adds BIU convenience.

### Gourinchas, Ray & Vayanos (2022), "A Preferred-Habitat Model of Term Premia, Exchange Rates, and Monetary Policy Spillovers," NBER WP 29875.
Continuous-time two-country **preferred-habitat** model (Vayanos-Vila lineage). Three investor types: **currency traders** (exogenous demand for foreign currency), **bond clienteles** (habitat demand for specific country/maturity bonds), and **global arbitrageurs** with limited (mean-variance) risk-bearing who span currencies and bonds. Arbitrageurs being the marginal investor in all markets *tightly links* UIP violations and Expectations-Hypothesis (term-premium) violations. QE (long-bond purchases) lowers domestic *and* foreign yields and depreciates the currency; conventional monetary policy transmits internationally but more weakly than QE. Joint determination of term premia and currency premia — relevant to KL2025 and to the maturity-dependence of carry.
- (a) **EXOGENOUS** — habitat/clientele demands and currency-trader demand are exogenous; arbitrageurs limited.
- (b) Open, 2-country, continuous time, infinite horizon.
- (c) No dollar-specific convenience; segmentation across maturities/currencies.
- (d) Joint term + currency premia; QE spillovers; carry depends on maturity and yield-curve slope.
- (e) **Relative** (premia between bonds/currencies); convenience as a habitat wedge, not absolute safe-asset level.
- **Proximity: 3.**

### Greenwood, Hanson, Stein & Sunderam (2023), "A Quantity-Driven Theory of Term Premia and Exchange Rates," *QJE* 138(4):2327–2389.
Specialized bond investors must absorb **supply/demand shocks for long-term bonds in two currencies**. Because long-term bonds and FX both load on short-rate surprises, a shift in long-bond supply in one currency moves the exchange rate *and* term premia in both. Matches the comovement of exchange rates and term premia, QE effects on FX, and (in an extension) persistent post-2008 CIP deviations. A **quantity theory**: relative supplies of safe long-term claims drive premia and the exchange rate.
- (a) **EXOGENOUS** — bond supply/demand shocks; arbitrageurs with limited capacity.
- (b) Open, 2-country, segmented bond markets.
- (c) No convenience-utility; relative *quantities* of long bonds matter.
- (d) Term + currency premia; QE → exchange rate; CIP deviations from quantities.
- (e) **Relative** (cross-currency quantities/premia).
- **Proximity: 3.** Connects "quantity of safe assets" to FX — relevant to big_cy's "not enough dollar debt" argument.

### Kekre & Lenel (2025), "A Model of U.S. Monetary Policy and the Global Financial Cycle," NBER WP / November 2025.
Multi-country NK open-economy model with **global intermediaries** facilitating capital flows and pricing assets; households trade only local-currency one-period bonds freely, intermediaries trade all assets (mean-variance, declining effective risk aversion in wealth). Key mechanism: intermediaries are **short the dollar** on average, so a US tightening appreciates the dollar, erodes intermediary net worth, and **raises the global price of risk** — the GFC operating through risk premia, not just rates. Disciplined to match US-monetary-surprise effects on the cross-section of international asset prices. Notably anticipates that **lower foreign demand for dollar assets** in the future would shrink intermediaries' dollar funding and *dampen* US spillovers — directly engages the "absolute level / quantity of dollar demand" theme.
- (a) **EXOGENOUS** at the household level (local-bond habitat); intermediary risk-bearing endogenous in wealth; no explicit BIU convenience here (distinct from KL2024).
- (b) Open, multi-country, NK, infinite horizon.
- (c) Dollar role via intermediary short-dollar positioning + risk pricing; convenience implicit through dollar funding demand.
- (d) US MP → global risk price; carry-relevant intermediary positioning; spillovers fall if dollar demand falls.
- (e) **Relative** risk premia; but the comparative-statics on "foreign demand for dollar assets" speak to the absolute-level question.
- **Proximity: 4.** Same authors/lineage; complementary mechanism (risk-pricing vs. convenience BIU).

### Kekre & Lenel (2022), "Monetary Policy, Redistribution, and Risk Premia," *Econometrica* 90(5):2249–2282.
Closed-economy HANK with **heterogeneity in marginal propensity to take risk (MPR)**: expansionary MP redistributes to high-MPR (risk-tolerant) households, lowering the risk premium and amplifying transmission 1.3–1.4×. Not international, but it is the **methodological engine** for KL2024/KL2025 — Epstein-Zin, heterogeneous risk-bearing, risk-premium movements from redistribution/intermediary wealth — and the source of the risk-bearing-capacity machinery big_cy must wrangle.
- (a) n/a (no safe-asset convenience); risk-bearing heterogeneity endogenous to portfolios.
- (b) **Closed** economy, HANK, infinite horizon.
- (c) n/a.
- (d) Risk premia respond to MP via redistribution; foundational for risk-bearing-capacity modeling.
- (e) n/a.
- **Proximity: 2.** Background method for the KL toolkit.

### Kekre & Lenel (2023), "The High Frequency Effects of Dollar Swap Lines," WP December 2023.
Empirical companion to KL2024. High-frequency identification around swap-line announcements: expanded dollar swap lines **reduce liquidity premia, compress CIP deviations, depreciate the dollar**, raise equities, lower VIX. Cross-section implies swap lines move the *dollar factor / price of risk*. Provides the identified moments KL2024 uses to calibrate the swap-line (safe-dollar-supply) experiment.
- (a) Empirical; treats swap lines as a **supply** shock to safe dollar liquidity (the mirror of an exogenous demand shock).
- (b) Open; high-frequency event study.
- (c) Dollar liquidity supply.
- (d) Swap lines ↓ convenience/liquidity premium, ↓ dollar, ↑ risky assets.
- (e) Speaks to the **level** of the dollar convenience/liquidity premium via supply shifts.
- **Proximity: 2.** Identification/empirical support for the safe-dollar-supply channel.

## CATEGORY C — Reserve-currency / dominant-currency models turning on safe-asset supply or oligopoly

### Farhi & Maggiori (2017), "A Model of the International Monetary System," *QJE* 133(1):295–355.
A formal IMS model with three attributes: supply & demand for reserve assets, exchange-rate regime, and institutions. **Demand for reserves is exogenous** (RoW mean-variance investors, elastic supply of risky assets, but safe reserve assets in limited supply). Reserve assets issued by a **monopoly hegemon** or **oligopoly of reserve countries (Cournot)**; issuers face an ex-post incentive to devalue/inflate, so the safe asset embeds a no-devaluation promise and admits **self-fulfilling confidence crises (à la Calvo)**. Rationalizes the **Triffin dilemma** (fragility grows with gross external debt; hegemon may over- or under-issue), the emergence of multipolarity (and the Nurkse warning it is more unstable), and ZLB-recessionary safe-asset scarcity (Keynesian). The hegemon earns a **positive endogenous safety premium / monopoly rent** on reserves.
- (a) **EXOGENOUS** reserve demand (RoW); supply is strategic (monopoly/oligopoly issuance).
- (b) Open, multi-country, with confidence-crisis dynamics.
- (c) Safety = no-devaluation commitment + fiscal capacity; convenience = endogenous safety premium / monopoly rent.
- (d) Hegemon issues safe debt at a premium; Triffin fragility; oligopoly competition lowers rents; ZLB scarcity is recessionary.
- (e) **Absolute** — an endogenous safety *premium* (level) on reserves, tied to issuer commitment, not a near-substitute basis.
- **Proximity: 4.** The supply-side/oligopoly counterpart to big_cy's demand-side convenience story.

### Choi, Kirpalani & Perez (2022), "The Macroeconomic Implications of US Market Power in Safe Assets," WP October 2022.
Two-country (US/RoW) dynamic borrowing-lending model where **US public debt yields a nonpecuniary convenience benefit** (BIU-style, can stand in for collateral/liquidity), and the US is the **monopoly provider** facing a downward-sloping demand. Asks whether the US *internalizes* its market power. Uses demand-elasticity rotations across high/low-volatility regimes (Bresnahan / Rivers-Vuong conduct test) and **rejects price-taking in favor of monopoly**. Monopoly → underprovision of safe assets (~half the price-taker supply), a markup of ~2/3 in the convenience yield, large US welfare gains / RoW losses. Studies entry of competing safe-asset suppliers.
- (a) **EXOGENOUS** convenience (nonpecuniary benefit on US public debt, BIU-style); **strategic supply** (monopoly).
- (b) Open, 2-country, dynamic, infinite horizon.
- (c) Nonpecuniary value of US safe public debt; level set partly by monopoly markup.
- (d) Underprovision of safe assets; markup in convenience yield; quantifies exorbitant privilege; portfolio = US issues scarce safe debt.
- (e) **Absolute** — the convenience yield is modeled as an absolute spread of US debt over safe capital, decomposed into nonpecuniary value + markup.
- **Proximity: 4.** Directly about the *level* of the convenience yield (markup) and safe-asset scarcity — close to big_cy's "not enough dollar debt."

### Coppola, Krishnamurthy & Xu (2023), "Liquidity, Debt Denomination, and Currency Dominance," NBER WP 30984.
**Endogenous** theory of *why one currency dominates debt denomination*. Firms must trade revenue for settlement assets; with **endogenous search frictions (Duffie-Gärleanu-Pedersen)** in asset markets, firms denominate debt in the unit of the asset that is *easiest to obtain* — the one with the largest safe, liquid short-term float. A **positive feedback loop** (dollar debt begets dollar debt, raising its liquidity) yields a single dominant currency. The dollar's edge: large safe, short-term, government-backed float (US fiscal capacity). Maps to historical regime shifts (Dutch florin → sterling → dollar; potential RMB rise).
- (a) **ENDOGENOUS** — convenience/liquidity arises from search frictions and a self-reinforcing float; demand emerges from settlement needs.
- (b) Open, multi-issuer, search-theoretic.
- (c) Liquidity of the safe short-term dollar float; government backing.
- (d) Currency dominance / dollar debt denomination; complementarity in issuance; debt composition skews to dollar.
- (e) **Absolute** — a liquidity premium (convenience yield) on the dominant currency's float, level-dependent and self-reinforcing.
- **Proximity: 4.** Microfounds dollar-debt dominance — relevant to big_cy's home-currency-bias and dollar-debt-supply themes.

### Gopinath & Stein (2021), "Banking, Trade, and the Making of a Dominant Currency," *QJE* 136(2):783–830.
Links **trade invoicing** and **safe-asset / deposit demand**: if imports are invoiced (and sticky) in dollars, households/firms prefer dollar deposits (safest in real terms for their consumption), banks fund in dollars, and firms borrow in dollars — a **complementarity** that makes a single currency dominant in both invoicing and finance even among similar candidates. EM firms endogenously take on currency mismatch; dollar safe assets carry lower expected returns → exorbitant privilege.
- (a) **ENDOGENOUS** — dollar convenience emerges from the invoicing↔safe-deposit↔banking complementarity.
- (b) Open, multi-country.
- (c) Dollar = unit of account for trade → safest store of value for consumption → dominant funding currency.
- (d) Dollar invoicing + dollar banking; EM currency mismatch; dollar exorbitant privilege / low expected returns.
- (e) **Absolute-leaning** — a convenience/safety premium on dollar deposits arising from invoicing, not a basis gap.
- **Proximity: 3.**

## CATEGORY D — Safe-asset shortage / safety-trap lineage (demand for safe assets, macro/ZLB)

### Caballero, Farhi & Gourinchas (2008), "An Equilibrium Model of 'Global Imbalances' and Low Interest Rates," *AER* 98(1):358–393.
Foundational **asset-supply** view of global imbalances. Regions differ in their *capacity to supply (safe) financial assets* from real investment. Fast-growing, financially underdeveloped regions (R, incl. emerging Asia) demand store-of-value assets they cannot produce; the US (U) supplies them. Generates persistent US current-account deficits, secularly low real rates ("Greenspan conundrum"), and a rising US share of global portfolios — as equilibrium, not anomaly. Extensions add FDI/investment and exchange-rate/excess-return implications.
- (a) **EXOGENOUS** demand for stores of value driven by R's inability to produce assets; no convenience utility per se.
- (b) Open, multi-region, dynamic.
- (c) US's superior capacity to *manufacture* financial assets (esp. safe ones).
- (d) US deficits; low world rate; US-tilted global portfolios; FDI/exchange-rate excess returns.
- (e) **Quantity/shortage** framing — relative scarcity of safe-asset supply, not a measured convenience wedge.
- **Proximity: 2.** Lineage/background for the safe-asset-shortage view big_cy inherits.

### Caballero & Farhi (2018), "The Safety Trap," *ReStud* 85(1):223–274.
Closed-/global-economy model of **safe-asset shortage at the ZLB**. When the demand for safe assets exceeds supply and the safe real rate cannot fall enough (ZLB), the economy enters a **"safety trap"** — a persistent, demand-driven recession distinct from a standard liquidity trap. QE that swaps safe for risky assets, and public safe-debt issuance, are expansionary. Establishes the macro consequences of an excess *demand* for safety.
- (a) **EXOGENOUS** demand for safe assets (preference for safety); supply constrained.
- (b) Closed/aggregate (global), with ZLB.
- (c) Safety per se (riskless store of value).
- (d) ZLB recessions from safe-asset scarcity; QE and safe public debt expansionary.
- (e) **Absolute** in spirit — a shortage in the *level* of safe assets drives a quantity/price wedge.
- **Proximity: 2.**

### Caballero, Farhi & Gourinchas (2021), "Global Imbalances and Policy Wars at the Zero Lower Bound," *ReStud* 88(6):2570–2621.
Open-economy extension of the safety-trap logic. With integrated but heterogeneous capital markets, nominal rigidities, and an effective lower bound, **liquidity/safety traps spill across borders**: surplus countries pull others into traps; the **reserve currency bears a disproportionate share** of the global trap (a "reserve-currency paradox" of its own); devaluations are beggar-thy-neighbor; safe public debt and government spending are globally expansionary. KL2024 cites this as the closest antecedent for the *effects of safety shocks*, extending it to non-binding ZLB with risk premia and portfolios.
- (a) **EXOGENOUS** safe-asset demand; ZLB / nominal rigidity.
- (b) Open, multi-country, ZLB.
- (c) Safety demand; reserve currency special by absorbing global trap.
- (d) Cross-border trap spillovers; reserve-currency disadvantage at ZLB; policy-war analysis.
- (e) **Absolute/quantity** safe-asset shortage framing.
- **Proximity: 3.** Direct antecedent to KL2024's safety-shock effects.

### He, Krishnamurthy & Milbradt (2019), "A Model of Safe Asset Determination," *AER* 109(4):1230–1262.
**Endogenous determination of which asset is "safe."** Safety is a **coordination/equilibrium-multiplicity** outcome: investors flee to the asset they expect others to treat as safe, and a government's debt is safe partly because of fiscal capacity *and* because investors coordinate on it. Generates the possibility that the US can be the world's safe asset even with comparable fundamentals to others (a "safe-asset" status that is self-fulfilling and tied to size/depth). Provides microfoundations for *why the dollar* and not another currency.
- (a) **ENDOGENOUS** — safety from coordination + fiscal capacity; demand for safety is primitive but *which* asset is safe is endogenous.
- (b) Open/global, with strategic/coordination structure.
- (c) Self-fulfilling safe-asset status + fundamentals (fiscal capacity, size).
- (d) Determines the identity of the global safe asset; flight-to-safety dynamics.
- (e) **Absolute** — about the existence/level of a safe-asset premium and who captures it.
- **Proximity: 3.** Microfoundation for dollar/Treasury safety that big_cy's exogenous-convenience shortcut sidesteps.

## CATEGORY E — Demand-system / quantitative structural-estimation approaches

### Koijen & Yogo (2020), "Exchange Rates and Asset Prices in a Global Demand System," NBER WP 27342.
Estimates a **demand system for financial assets across 36 countries** (short-term debt, long-term debt, equity), built on the Koijen-Yogo characteristics-based portfolio-choice approach, matching IMF CPIS holdings jointly with prices via market clearing. Macro/policy variables (short rates, debt quantities, FX reserves) explain 55% of exchange-rate, 57% of long-yield, and 69% of stock-price variation. Estimates an **average convenience yield of 2.15% on US long-term debt and 1.70% on US equity** — a quantitative, holdings-based estimate of the *absolute* US convenience yield, and evidence of market segmentation / inelastic demand.
- (a) **EXOGENOUS / estimated** latent demand (characteristics-based); convenience yield recovered as a residual asset characteristic.
- (b) Open, 36-country, structural demand estimation.
- (c) Convenience on US assets estimated directly (a US-asset characteristic).
- (d) Decomposes FX / yields / equity variation; quantifies US convenience yield; segmentation matters for FX.
- (e) **Absolute** — directly estimates the *level* of the US convenience yield (2.15% debt, 1.70% equity). Highly relevant to big_cy's magnitude question.
- **Proximity: 4.**

### Koijen & Yogo (2019), "A Demand System Approach to Asset Pricing," *JPE* 127(4):1475–1515.
The **methodological foundation** for demand-system asset pricing. Characteristics-based asset demand from constrained portfolio choice with heterogeneous beliefs and short-sale constraints; mean-variance portfolios as logit weights on observed characteristics + latent demand; IV estimation (market-clearing instrument) to handle price-demand endogeneity. Closed-economy (US equities) illustration. The engine behind Koijen-Yogo (2020) and Jiang-Richmond-Zhang (2022).
- (a) **EXOGENOUS / estimated** latent demand + characteristics.
- (b) Closed (US equities) illustration; method is general.
- (c) n/a (general asset pricing).
- (d) Inelastic demand, institutional holdings drive prices/volatility.
- (e) n/a (method).
- **Proximity: 1.** Background method.

### Jiang, Richmond & Zhang (2022), "A Portfolio Approach to Global Imbalances," WP June 2022.
Adapts the Koijen-Yogo demand system to **decompose the US NFA position and the exorbitant-privilege reversal**. Three findings: (i) countervailing forces (savings gluts + issuance + MP widen the US NFA; demand shifts partly offset) so no single-channel theory works quantitatively; (ii) a shift in investor demand toward US *equities* raised realized returns on US liabilities and drove the **post-2010 reversal of privilege**; (iii) a novel **quantity dimension of privilege**: the US can issue much more long-term debt per unit rise in yields than other developed economies (reverses when scaled by GDP).
- (a) **EXOGENOUS / estimated** demand (demand-system).
- (b) Open, multi-country, structural-estimation of holdings + prices.
- (c) US-asset demand/quality characteristics (incl. convenience) estimated.
- (d) Drivers of US NFA; privilege reversal from equity-demand shift; quantity dimension of privilege.
- (e) **Absolute/quantity** — measures the level/quantity dimension of US privilege.
- **Proximity: 4.** Empirically closest to big_cy's NFA-composition target facts (intellectual cousin to the `rier` backbone).

### Clayton, Maggiori & Schreger (2024), "A Theory of Economic Coercion and Fragmentation," NBER WP 33309 (BIS WP 1224).
Geoeconomics: great powers (US/China) wield financial/trade integration as a coercion tool by threatening to suspend relationships; the dollar's centrality in safe assets/payments is a *source of power*. Smaller countries pursue anti-coercion policy / fragmentation. Tangential to the modeling-of-convenience question but relevant to the strategic/durability dimension of dollar dominance.

- (b) Open, multi-country, strategic.
- (c) Dollar/financial-network centrality as leverage.
- (d) Fragmentation, anti-coercion; durability of dollar dominance.
- (e) n/a.
- **Proximity: 1.** Background on the strategic side of dollar dominance.

## Cross-cutting foundational reference (the map)

### Maggiori (2022), "International Macroeconomics with Imperfect Financial Markets," *Handbook of International Economics* vol. 6, ch. 5, pp. 199–236.
The **survey/map** of the financial-frictions view of open-economy macro. Lays out the canonical financier/portfolio-balance structure (Gabaix-Maggiori), the role of risk-bearing capacity Γ, UIP-vs-CIP distinctions, FX intervention, the carry trade, multi-asset extensions (term + currency premia), and the asymmetric/US-special branch (Farhi-Maggiori, He et al., Gopinath-Stein). Reads as the organizing taxonomy for the whole strand and explicitly flags the convenience-yield / safe-asset sub-literature and the open question of what *drives* the flows.
- (a) Surveys both exogenous and endogenous demand.
- (b) Open (general).
- (c) Maps the alternative sources of dollar specialness.
- (d) Comprehensive on FX/portfolios/carry under frictions.
- (e) Discusses both relative (basis) and absolute (convenience) notions.
- **Proximity: 2.** Essential orientation; not a competing model.
